Key active

Niacinamide

A form of vitamin B3 that signals skin cells to slow sebum production, strengthen the lipid barrier, and reduce the visible appearance of pores. Also inhibits melanin transfer — which is why it fades post-acne marks over time.

How it works

B3 boosts ceramide production at the cellular level, repairing the moisture barrier. It also slows the transfer of melanosomes from melanocytes to keratinocytes — slowing new pigmentation.

Best used

AM and PM, applied to clean skin before your moisturiser or SPF. Plays nicely with vitamin C, retinol, and exfoliating acids — despite the old myth that you can't layer it.

Clinical evidence

Effective at 2–10% concentration. Our formula sits in the clinically meaningful range — not the symbolic 'trace amount' on most labels.— J Cosmet Dermatol, 2004 & 2017

Skin types it suits

Universal — works for oily, combination, dry, and sensitive skin. Especially impactful for combination + post-acne marks, which is why it's our most-recommended active.

Common questions

Frequently asked.

When will I see results? +

Most people notice less shine and clearer pores within 2–3 weeks. Tone-correction (post-acne marks) is slower — give it 6–8 weeks of consistent use to see fading.

Can I layer this with retinol or vitamin C? +

Yes — there's a persistent online myth that you can't, but it's been disproven multiple times. We recommend niacinamide gel first (lighter), then your retinol or vitamin C on top.

Is it safe during pregnancy? +

Niacinamide is generally considered pregnancy-safe — it's water-soluble vitamin B3, with no known risks. We always recommend confirming with your doctor before adding any new product during pregnancy.

Will it break me out? +

Niacinamide gel is non-comedogenic and fragrance-free. Most blemish-prone customers tolerate it well. If you're highly reactive, patch-test on your jawline for a few days first.
